HEARCLIP: Personalization of Hearing Aids through Bayesian Preference Elicitation

Modern hearing aids have evolved into complex digital signal processing (DSP) devices. The tuning of DSP parameters so as to maximize patient satisfaction (a.k.a. fitting) of a hearing aid, has become similarly complex. Several factors contribute to the complexity of this optimization task, including:

  • the dimensionality of the parameter space is large;
  • the determinants of hearing-impaired user satisfaction are unknown;
  • the evaluation of this satisfaction through listening tests is costly (in terms of patient burden and clinical time investment) and unreliable (due to inconsistent responses).
As a result, the vast majority of the many parameters of a hearing aid algorithm are fixed by the manufacturer at a 'best guess' value. Just a few parameters are personalized by the hearing aid dispenser based on the nature of the hearing loss. Thus, fitting of hearing aids relies more on empirical procedures than on mathematical optimization methods.

In February 2007 we started a research project on developing efficient hearing aid fitting algorithms. The project is funded by the Dutch funding agency STW (Foundation for Technological Sciences) and the official web page for the project is here. On this website, we describe the problem statement and provide a list of our downloadable publications.

The goal of the project is to improve the hearing aid fitting procedure. We take a Bayesian Decision theoretic approach. If we knew what determines patient satisfaction, the task would be easy. Since we do not know this, we model the uncertainty about patient satisfaction through probability distributions over utility models. Using listening tests of the type 'which of the two audio samples sounds best?' we elicit responses from the user. Combining patient responses with a probabilistic utility model in a Bayesian fashion, we are able to learn more about the determinants of patient satisfaction. A second feature of the Bayesian method is the possibility to select the best next listening experiment throughout a fitting session. Learning about the patient satisfaction model in the HearClip fitting process is similar to the decision-theoretic process of incremental utility elicitation. In the second stage of HearClip fitting, we find the tuning parameters that optimize the utilities, averaged over a data base of relevant audio samples. This process is known as expected utility maximization and involves research into efficient optimization algorithms. The proposed method is new to the hearing aid domain but can also be applied to perceptual optimization tasks that are unrelated to hearing aids, e.g. tuning of image processing algorithms. The framework and some initial research results have been laid out by us through a few publications, see the literature list below.
